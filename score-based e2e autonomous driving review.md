# Review of score-based end-to-end autonomous driving

This note reviews the papers collected in [[score-based e2e autonomous driving papers 2022-2026]].

## 1. Scope and definition

In this review, **score-based end-to-end autonomous driving** uses a broad but useful definition:
- the system predicts or samples **multiple candidate trajectories**,
- then uses a learned mechanism to **score, rank, or refine-select** them,
- instead of regressing a single future path directly.

Under this definition, the field contains three closely related families:
1. **pure scoring / selection** over static or factorized vocabularies;
2. **proposal + scoring hybrids** where candidates are generated by anchors, refinement, or diffusion and then selected;
3. **diffusion / flow-matching planners** that still keep an explicit selection, ranking, or RL-guided quality-control stage.

The core paper list is in [[score-based e2e autonomous driving papers 2022-2026]].

## 2. Historical development

### 2.1 Before the score-based wave
Earlier end-to-end planners often regressed a single trajectory directly. This simplified training, but it was fundamentally mismatched with real driving, where multiple behaviors may all be valid. Open-loop imitation also made it hard to reason about safety-critical edge cases.

### 2.2 The first clear score-based formulation
[[Hydra-MDP 2406]] is the clearest early milestone in this line. Its key move is conceptual: **planning becomes candidate generation plus learned multi-target scoring**, trained not only from human demonstrations but also from rule-based simulator teachers. This turns safety, progress, and comfort from post-hoc heuristics into differentiable supervision targets.

At roughly the same time, [[SparseDrive 2405]] showed that multimodal planning and final selection could be done efficiently on top of sparse scene representations. Compared with Hydra-MDP, SparseDrive is less explicitly teacher-distilled, but it already treats planning as a **multimodal select-and-rerank** problem.

### 2.3 Expansion into generation + scoring
After the first vocabulary-scoring methods, the field quickly split.

One branch deepened the **strict score-based** paradigm:
- [[Hydra-MDP++ 2503]] improves the teacher targets,
- [[Hydra-NeXt 2503]] pushes the Hydra family toward stronger closed-loop transfer,
- [[HMAD 2505]], [[DriveSuprim 2506]], [[Generalized Trajectory Scoring 2506]], [[ZTRS 2510]], [[Navigation Compliance Gap 2512]], and [[SparseDriveV2 2603]] all refine the proposition that **selection quality** is the real bottleneck.

A second branch argued that better **candidate generation** is needed:
- [[DiffusionDrive 2411]] shows truncated anchor-based diffusion can be fast and strong,
- [[ComDrive 2410]] adds comfort-oriented diffusion + scoring,
- [[GoalFlow 2503]], [[GuideFlow 2511]], and [[Adaptive Time Step Flow Matching 2602]] use flow matching,
- [[CdDrive 2602]], [[ReCogDrive 2506]], [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]], [[HAD 2604]], [[HDP 2602]], and [[FeaXDrive 2604]] all treat generation quality and sampling-time guidance as first-class concerns.

### 2.4 The current phase: scaling and hybridization
The current literature does not converge on one universal architecture. Instead, it converges on one principle: **multimodality must be modeled explicitly**, and the planner needs a quality-control mechanism more structured than plain imitation.

The most explicit “pure scoring” claim is [[SparseDriveV2 2603]], whose title — *Scoring is All You Need* — crystallizes one side of the debate. The strongest counter-position comes from hybrid generative methods such as [[DiffusionDrive 2411]], [[GoalFlow 2503]], [[TransDiffuser 2505]], and [[HDP 2602]], which argue that better candidate generation remains essential.

## 3. Taxonomy of methods

### 3.1 Pure scoring over static or structured candidate sets
Representative papers:
- [[Hydra-MDP 2406]]
- [[Hydra-MDP++ 2503]]
- [[DriveSuprim 2506]]
- [[ZTRS 2510]]
- [[SparseDriveV2 2603]]

Common pattern:
- build a fixed or factorized candidate set,
- score each candidate with learned metrics,
- choose the highest-quality trajectory.

Strengths:
- interpretable candidate-selection structure,
- easy to attach simulator-aligned metrics,
- often easier to stabilize than free-form generative planners,
- especially strong when candidate coverage is high.

Weaknesses:
- fundamentally limited by candidate coverage,
- difficult to adapt if the right behavior is not in the set,
- dense vocabularies increase memory and scoring cost.

### 3.2 Proposal + scoring hybrids
Representative papers:
- [[HMAD 2505]]
- [[Generalized Trajectory Scoring 2506]]
- [[CdDrive 2602]]
- [[ComDrive 2410]]

Common pattern:
- generate candidates through anchors, refinement, or diffusion,
- keep a learned scorer as the final decision-maker.

Strengths:
- better local adaptation than fixed vocabularies,
- preserves interpretability of explicit selection,
- often a good compromise between expressiveness and control.

Weaknesses:
- pipeline complexity grows quickly,
- proposals and scorer can become mismatched,
- improvements can be hard to attribute cleanly.

### 3.3 Diffusion / flow-matching dominant planners
Representative papers:
- [[DiffusionDrive 2411]]
- [[GoalFlow 2503]]
- [[GuideFlow 2511]]
- [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]]
- [[HAD 2604]]
- [[FeaXDrive 2604]]
- [[HDP 2602]]

Common pattern:
- use diffusion or flow matching to generate multimodal trajectories,
- then improve quality with scoring, guidance, hierarchy, or RL.

Strengths:
- naturally expressive multimodal trajectory generation,
- easier to incorporate style, constraint, or semantic conditions,
- can avoid coarse discretization artifacts.

Weaknesses:
- higher inference complexity unless aggressively optimized,
- training can be unstable or mode-collapsed,
- often needs extra guidance, anchors, or RL to become reliable.

## 4. The main design axes in the literature

### 4.1 Candidate source: static coverage vs adaptive generation
A central debate runs through nearly all of these papers.

**Position A: if coverage is dense enough, static scoring is enough.**  
This is the thesis of [[SparseDriveV2 2603]], and it is already foreshadowed by [[Hydra-MDP 2406]] and [[DriveSuprim 2506]]. The claim is that candidate selection fails mainly because the vocabulary is too sparse or the scorer is too weak.

**Position B: static vocabularies cannot fully capture interaction-heavy edge cases.**  
This motivates [[DiffusionDrive 2411]], [[CdDrive 2602]], [[GoalFlow 2503]], and [[TransDiffuser 2505]], where dynamic generation creates finer-grained or more adaptive proposals.

My synthesis: **both camps are partly right**. In routine driving, dense scoring over a structured vocabulary is often sufficient and computationally attractive. In highly interactive or unusual scenes, adaptive generation appears more important.

### 4.2 What is being scored?
Different papers score different things:
- distance to expert behavior,
- collision and drivable-area compliance,
- progress,
- comfort,
- navigation compliance,
- feasibility in trajectory space,
- or decomposed multi-objective reward components.

This evolution matters. [[Hydra-MDP 2406]] made simulator metrics central. [[Hydra-MDP++ 2503]] broadens them. [[Navigation Compliance Gap 2512]] shows benchmark score alone can miss route obedience. [[FeaXDrive 2604]] argues that **trajectory-space feasibility** is itself an under-modeled target.

The field has therefore moved from “score the most likely expert-like trajectory” to “score the trajectory that best trades off safety, progress, comfort, route compliance, and physical plausibility.”

### 4.3 Open-loop optimization vs closed-loop reality
This remains one of the field’s biggest unresolved issues.

- [[Hydra-NeXt 2503]] explicitly targets the open-loop / closed-loop gap.
- [[DriveSuprim 2506]] and [[SparseDriveV2 2603]] show strong benchmark performance on both open-loop and closed-loop settings, but still depend heavily on offline supervision.
- [[ZTRS 2510]], [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]], [[ReCogDrive 2506]], [[HAD 2604]], and [[FeaXDrive 2604]] all move toward RL or post-training.

The trend is clear: **pure imitation is no longer considered sufficient**. Even when training begins with imitation, most strong recent methods add some form of simulator-guided selection, RL, or rule-aware refinement.

### 4.4 Representation of planning uncertainty
Different papers encode uncertainty in different ways:
- discrete trajectory vocabularies ([[Hydra-MDP 2406]], [[Hydra-MDP++ 2503]]),
- factorized path/velocity vocabularies ([[SparseDriveV2 2603]]),
- anchor-conditioned denoising ([[DiffusionDrive 2411]], [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]]),
- goal-conditioned generation ([[GoalFlow 2503]]),
- flow matching with explicit constraints ([[GuideFlow 2511]]),
- trajectory-centric diffusion with feasibility guidance ([[FeaXDrive 2604]]).

This is not just an implementation detail. It changes what kinds of failures the model can express and correct.

## 5. What the strongest papers are really saying

### 5.1 Hydra-MDP and descendants: planning is learned ranking
The Hydra line says the planner should not output “the answer” directly. It should learn to **rank candidate futures** using richer supervisory signals than simple imitation. This is the intellectual origin of much of the later score-based literature.

### 5.2 DriveSuprim and SparseDriveV2: scoring is still underpowered
[[DriveSuprim 2506]] and [[SparseDriveV2 2603]] argue that the field may have moved too quickly toward more elaborate generators. Their position is that **selection precision and candidate coverage** remain the real bottlenecks.

This is a strong thesis because it is empirically competitive. It suggests that even if generative planners are elegant, large gains are still available inside a simpler score-based formulation.

### 5.3 DiffusionDrive and successors: generation quality matters
The diffusion line counters that pure static selection is too rigid. [[DiffusionDrive 2411]] shows real-time diffusion can work. [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]] shows RL can stabilize multimodal quality. [[HAD 2604]] adds hierarchy to make search easier. [[FeaXDrive 2604]] argues quality should be judged in trajectory space, not just benchmark metric space.

The diffusion literature therefore broadens the notion of “score-based” from candidate ranking into a larger **generate, guide, and optimize** pipeline.

### 5.4 Hybrid papers show the field is converging pragmatically
Several papers reject the idea that one must choose static scoring *or* dynamic generation:
- [[HMAD 2505]] mixes anchored proposals with simulator-supervised scoring,
- [[Generalized Trajectory Scoring 2506]] scores both static and diffusion candidates,
- [[CdDrive 2602]] keeps original anchors and adds adaptive refinement.

These papers are important because they imply the field’s practical answer may be **hybrid systems** rather than ideological purity.

## 6. Benchmarks and what they bias the field toward

The main benchmark ecosystems in these papers are:
- **nuScenes**: useful, but mostly open-loop and easier to “look good” without true planning robustness;
- **NAVSIM / Navhard**: increasingly central for multimodal planning and score-based selection;
- **Bench2Drive**: important for closed-loop embodied performance;
- **HUGSIM / interPlan / WOMD**: provide additional stress tests, especially for generalization or motion-planning quality.

Benchmark choice strongly affects research direction.

- nuScenes papers often emphasize representation and open-loop error.
- NAVSIM papers emphasize planning-oriented scores and candidate quality.
- Bench2Drive/HUGSIM papers force the field to care about actual closed-loop success.

This is why papers like [[Hydra-NeXt 2503]], [[DriveSuprim 2506]], [[SparseDriveV2 2603]], [[ReCogDrive 2506]], and [[HAD 2604]] are especially important: they are harder to dismiss as purely open-loop improvements.

## 7. Strengths of the score-based paradigm

Across the literature, the score-based paradigm has several durable advantages.

### 7.1 Better alignment with multimodal driving
Driving is inherently multimodal. Score-based methods acknowledge this directly by comparing multiple plausible futures.

### 7.2 Natural interface for safety and rule metrics
It is easier to attach safety, comfort, route compliance, or feasibility signals to candidate trajectories than to a single regressed output.

### 7.3 Interpretability and controllability
Pure scorer-based systems often make it clearer **why** a trajectory was selected or rejected, especially compared with fully implicit generative models.

### 7.4 Compatibility with RL and teacher supervision
The same candidate space can support imitation, rule-based teachers, or RL fine-tuning. This flexibility is a major reason the paradigm has scaled well.

## 8. Weaknesses and unresolved problems

### 8.1 Candidate coverage remains a hard ceiling
If the right maneuver is absent, the best scorer in the world cannot recover it. This is the fundamental weakness of pure static-vocabulary methods.

### 8.2 Score design is still benchmark-shaped
Many papers rely on benchmark-specific metrics or challenge-specific scorers. This raises the risk of overfitting to what is measured rather than what is truly safe.

### 8.3 Closed-loop generalization is still fragile
Even the strongest open-loop scorers can fail under compounding closed-loop errors. The repeated appearance of RL, refinement, and control branches shows the problem is not solved.

### 8.4 System complexity is rising fast
The field began with relatively clean score-based ranking. Many newer systems combine:
- candidate generation,
- scoring,
- refinement,
- RL,
- route conditioning,
- VLM reasoning,
- or constraint guidance.

These systems are stronger, but harder to analyze and reproduce.

## 9. My synthesis

I think the literature now supports five conclusions.

### 9.1 The key shift was from regression to comparison
The most important conceptual advance is not diffusion or flow matching by itself. It is the move from **predict one future** to **compare many futures**.

### 9.2 Pure scoring is not obsolete
[[SparseDriveV2 2603]] and [[DriveSuprim 2506]] show that strong candidate structuring and strong ranking remain highly competitive. The field should not assume dynamic generation is always necessary.

### 9.3 But generation matters in hard interactions
At the same time, papers such as [[DiffusionDrive 2411]], [[CdDrive 2602]], [[GoalFlow 2503]], and [[FeaXDrive 2604]] show that fine-grained, scene-adaptive generation becomes more valuable when fixed vocabularies are too coarse.

### 9.4 The winning systems increasingly optimize *quality control*, not just proposal quality
Whether the candidates come from a vocabulary, anchors, diffusion, or flow matching, the strongest papers all invest heavily in some quality-control mechanism:
- better scorers,
- better teacher targets,
- RL post-training,
- hierarchy,
- feasibility guidance,
- or explicit compliance objectives.

### 9.5 The long-term winner will probably be hybrid
The literature increasingly suggests that the end state may be:
- a strong structured candidate prior,
- a learned adaptive generator for hard scenes,
- and a scorer / critic / RL objective that chooses among them under safety and route constraints.

In that sense, the strict opposition between “score-based” and “generative” may disappear. The field may converge on systems where **generation proposes and scoring governs**.

## 10. Open problems

The most important open problems I see are:

1. **Benchmark-independent scoring objectives**  
   How to avoid optimizing only the published metric bundle.

2. **Closed-loop training without brittle simulator overfitting**  
   RL and post-training help, but stability and transfer remain difficult.

3. **Better route- and intent-aware planning**  
   [[Navigation Compliance Gap 2512]] shows this is still under-addressed.

4. **Physical feasibility as a first-class target**  
   [[FeaXDrive 2604]] is a strong step, but the problem is broader.

5. **Model efficiency at deployment time**  
   Fast systems like [[DiffusionDrive 2411]], [[RAPiD 2602]], and adaptive flow methods show this is possible, but not yet solved universally.

6. **Interpretability of hybrid systems**  
   As systems add VLMs, RL, hierarchy, and guidance, it becomes harder to understand which component is responsible for which behavior.

## 11. Practical reading order

If I wanted to understand this area efficiently, I would read in this order:

1. [[Hydra-MDP 2406]] — the canonical early score-based formulation  
2. [[SparseDrive 2405]] — sparse perception + multimodal planning  
3. [[Hydra-MDP++ 2503]] — richer teacher targets  
4. [[DriveSuprim 2506]] — why selection precision matters  
5. [[SparseDriveV2 2603]] — the strongest pure-scoring statement  
6. [[DiffusionDrive 2411]] — the key diffusion counterpart  
7. [[CdDrive 2602]] and [[Generalized Trajectory Scoring 2506]] — hybrid candidate-set design  
8. [[DiffusionDriveV2 2512]], [[HAD 2604]], [[FeaXDrive 2604]] — the modern diffusion / RL / feasibility line  
9. [[Navigation Compliance Gap 2512]] — route obedience blind spot  
10. [[ReCogDrive 2506]] / [[HDP 2602]] — broader system-level scaling and cognition directions

## 12. Bottom line

The bottom line is simple:

**Score-based end-to-end autonomous driving has become one of the most compelling planning paradigms because it matches the multimodal nature of driving, interfaces naturally with safety-aware supervision, and scales from static candidate ranking to modern hybrid generative planners.**

The main unresolved question is no longer whether score-based planning works. It is **which combination of candidate structure, generation mechanism, and quality-control objective gives the best trade-off between safety, controllability, robustness, and efficiency.**
